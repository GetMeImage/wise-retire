Retirement Planning for Salaried Employees: The Complete 2026 Guide

If you are salaried in India, you already have the best retirement accounts in the world — EPF, NPS, PPF. Most people just don't use them together properly. This guide shows you exactly how to combine them for a tax-efficient, fully-funded retirement.

📅 March 2026⏱️ 12 min read📋 All salaried employees

Your 3-Account Foundation

Every Indian salaried employee has automatic access to three extraordinary retirement accounts. Most people treat them as separate — the real power is in using them as a system:

The Ideal Monthly Allocation by Salary

For ₹1 lakh/month take-home salary:

EPF (auto-deducted from gross): ~₹7,200/month (12% of ₹60K basic)
PPF: ₹12,500/month (maxing the ₹1.5L/year limit)
NPS voluntary: ₹4,200/month (₹50K/year for 80CCD(1B) deduction — saves ₹15K/yr in old regime)
Equity MF SIP: ₹15,000–20,000/month (your primary growth engine)
Emergency fund + term insurance: ₹5,000–8,000/month

Total retirement saving: ~₹40,000/month = 40% savings rate — excellent.
For ₹2 lakh/month take-home salary:

EPF (auto): ~₹14,400/month
PPF: ₹12,500/month (max)
NPS voluntary: ₹4,200/month (₹50K/year for tax deduction)
Equity MF SIP: ₹50,000–70,000/month
Emergency fund buffer: ₹10,000/month

Total retirement saving: ~₹91,000/month = 45% savings rate — on track for FIRE by 50.

Decade-by-Decade Action Plan

In Your 20s: Foundation

  • Open PPF immediately. Every year without PPF is a year of EEE compounding lost forever.
  • Start equity SIP — any amount. ₹2,000/month started at 22 is worth more than ₹20,000/month started at 42.
  • Buy term insurance (₹1–2 crore cover). Cheapest when young — ₹700–1,200/month for ₹1Cr cover at age 25.
  • Don't withdraw EPF when changing jobs. Transfer it. The compounding at 8.25% tax-free is irreplaceable.

In Your 30s: Growth

  • Step up SIP by 10–15% every year. Your salary is growing — your SIP should too.
  • Add NPS voluntary contribution (₹50K/year) for the additional 80CCD(1B) deduction — saves ₹15,600/year in taxes (30% slab).
  • Clear home loan aggressively — owning home debt-free before retirement reduces your corpus need by 35–40%.
  • Calculate your retirement number using RetireWise — knowing the target makes the saving intentional.

In Your 40s: Acceleration

  • Maximum EPF voluntary contribution (VPF). VPF gives 8.25% guaranteed, tax-free — better than any FD. You can contribute up to 100% of basic as VPF.
  • Review asset allocation. At 40, you can still afford 70–80% equity. Don't de-risk too early.
  • Check your NPS corpus trajectory — are you on track? Adjust NPS fund choice (equity %, lifecycle fund) if needed.
  • Build a separate healthcare corpus — ₹15–25 lakh in liquid funds. Medical costs spike in your 50s.

In Your 50s: Transition

  • Gradually shift equity MF to hybrid/balanced. Move 5–10% from equity to debt each year from age 55.
  • Max out NPS equity allocation (75%) — you still have 5–10 years of growth.
  • Decide on NPS annuity provider and compare rates — annuity rates vary significantly across providers.
  • Plan your withdrawal sequence — which account do you tap first? (EPF → PPF → MF → NPS)

Optimal Withdrawal Sequence in Retirement

The order in which you withdraw from different accounts has a massive impact on tax paid over your retirement:

SequenceAccountWhy This Order
1stEPF lump sum100% tax-free after 5 years. Take it all upfront. Reinvest into debt MF or FD if not needed immediately.
2ndPPF maturity + extensionsEEE — fully tax-free, zero LTCG, zero income tax. Extend in 5-year blocks and withdraw as needed.
3rdEquity MF (annual)Withdraw up to ₹1.25L gains/year tax-free. Plan withdrawals to stay below LTCG threshold annually.
4thNPS lump sum (60–80%)Take in a low-income year to minimise tax on the taxable 20% portion.
5th (ongoing)NPS annuityTaxable at slab — but by now other tax-free sources are exhausted. 87A rebate helps if income ≤₹12L.
Last resortFD / debt fundsMost tax-inefficient — fully taxable at slab. Keep minimum here; use primarily for emergency liquidity.

7 Most Common Retirement Mistakes Salaried Indians Make

  1. Withdrawing EPF on job change. You lose 8.25% tax-free compounding and reset the 5-year continuous service clock. Always transfer.
  2. Keeping salary in savings account. Savings account gives 3–4%. Any money not needed in 3 months should be in liquid MF or PPF.
  3. Treating EPF as the only retirement savings. EPF maximum contribution is capped by your basic salary. For most people it is 15–20% of what they actually need.
  4. Not buying term insurance early. A ₹1Cr term policy at 25 costs ₹700–800/month. At 40, it costs ₹2,500–3,500/month. The corpus protection it provides is irreplaceable.
  5. Pausing SIP during market falls. The worst thing to do — you are buying fewer units at lower prices when you should be buying more.
  6. Ignoring the new NPS 80CCD(1B) deduction. ₹50,000 extra NPS contribution = ₹15,600 saved in taxes at 30% slab + 4% cess. That's a guaranteed 31% return on ₹50K — better than any investment.
  7. No healthcare reserve. One serious hospitalisation at 65 without a dedicated reserve can wipe out 1–3 years of SIP savings.

How much of my salary should I save for retirement? ▼
A minimum of 20–25% of gross salary. EPF alone is typically 12% of basic — usually 7–8% of gross. Add PPF (₹12,500/month) and a modest SIP and you reach 20–25% easily. For FIRE or early retirement targets, 40–50% savings rate is needed. The higher your savings rate, the earlier you can retire — it's a direct relationship.
Is NPS worth it for a private sector employee? ▼
Yes — specifically the ₹50,000/year voluntary contribution under 80CCD(1B) is worth it for anyone in the 20% or 30% tax slab. It saves ₹10,400–15,600 in tax annually, and the NPS equity funds have delivered 10–12% returns historically. The 20% mandatory annuity at retirement is the only downside — but with the new 80% lump sum option, NPS is more flexible than ever.
